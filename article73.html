ITZY's Yuna Launches Solo Career With "Ice Cream" EP As "That's A No No" Goes Viral

March 23, 2026 9 min read By Chloe Nam-Davis

Call it perfect timing or cosmic alignment—ITZY's youngest member Yuna couldn't have picked a better moment to launch her solo career. As the group's 2020 B-side "That's a No No" experiences an unexpected viral resurgence, Yuna steps into the spotlight with her debut EP "Ice Cream," hoping to ride the wave of renewed attention all the way to solo stardom.

"I'm really grateful for 'That's a No No' regaining popularity," Yuna said during a press conference in eastern Seoul on Monday, ahead of the EP's release. "It got hyped up right before my solo promotions. It'd be wonderful if listeners would give love to not only 'That's A No No' but also 'Ice Cream.'"

The "That's A No No" Phenomenon

Before diving into Yuna's solo debut, it's worth understanding the remarkable circumstances surrounding it. ITZY's performance of "That's a No No"—a B-side from their 2020 EP "IT'z ME"—went viral after the group performed it during their "Tunnel Vision" world tour in February.

The synchronized choreography and distinctive dance moves captured the internet's attention, leading to a dramatic chart resurgence for the six-year-old song. A practice video for the song topped Korea's chart for most popular music-related videos on YouTube, while the song itself climbed from No. 961 to No. 157 on domestic streaming platform Melon between March 2 and 16.

The viral moment has reminded fans and casual listeners alike of ITZY's exceptional performance skills, creating a perfect backdrop for Yuna's solo venture. It's the kind of organic promotion that money can't buy and timing couldn't have planned better.

About "Ice Cream"

Set to be released at 6 p.m. KST on Monday, Yuna's first solo EP features four tracks. The lineup includes the title track "Ice Cream," along with B-sides "B-Boy," "Blue Maze," and "Hyper Dream."

The title track is described as a bubbly pop song, and its music video leans into cheeky, bright visuals. Yuna appears in blonde wigs and frozen poses to mimic the nature of ice cream—a playful concept that showcases a different side of the performer known for ITZY's fierce girl-crush image.

"It's a good song to listen to in the spring," Yuna explained during the press conference. She recalled how the melody had immediately hooked her, leading her to imagine how the song would look on stage. The visual performance aspect has always been central to Yuna's artistry, and she was eager to create a complete audiovisual experience for her debut.

The EP represents a conscious departure from ITZY's typical sound, allowing Yuna to explore musical territories that might not fit the group's established identity. This balance between solo experimentation and group coherence is a challenge all K-pop idols face when launching individual projects.

Following In Yeji's Footsteps

Yuna is the second ITZY member to pursue solo activities, following leader Yeji, who launched her solo career in March 2025. The staggered approach to solo debuts has become common practice among K-pop groups, allowing agencies to maximize promotional opportunities while maintaining group cohesion.

"Thanks to Yeji, who had such a wonderful solo debut, I was able to take on a new challenge as well," Yuna acknowledged. She revealed that she watched Yeji's solo performances as references while preparing for her own promotions, learning from her bandmate's experience navigating the transition from group member to solo artist.

ITZY debuted under JYP Entertainment in 2019 and quickly established themselves as one of the premier girl groups of their generation. Known for high-energy performances and solid live vocals, the quintet has built a devoted fanbase that eagerly supports both group activities and individual ventures.

The Pressure of Solo Debut

Despite the favorable circumstances, Yuna admitted to feeling significant pressure about her solo launch. "I feel the pressure of launching a solo album seven years after my debut, but I still really want to do this right," she said candidly.

The preparation process revealed aspects of herself that even Yuna hadn't fully recognized. "While preparing this album, I learned that I have a perfectionist side of me," she shared. "I sometimes felt so confident, but other times so small. During those moments, I wrote in my journal to better recognize my emotions."

This emotional honesty resonated with fans and journalists at the press conference, offering a glimpse behind the polished idol facade. The vulnerability of solo work—where there are no bandmates to share the burden or credit—can be both liberating and terrifying for artists accustomed to group dynamics.

Yuna also confessed that standing on her own made her miss her bandmates. The other ITZY members showed their support by sending snack trucks during the "Ice Cream" music video shoot—a common gesture of support in Korean entertainment industry.

Mentor Support

JYP Entertainment founder Park Jin-young, known for his hands-on approach to artist development, also offered guidance during Yuna's solo preparations. However, his advice was notably practical rather than artistic.

"He told me not to get injured and to finish promoting [the EP] in good health," Yuna reported. The seemingly simple advice reflects the grueling nature of K-pop promotions, where packed schedules of performances, interviews, and fan events can take a physical toll on artists.

Park's concern for artist health marks a shift from earlier eras of K-pop, when the industry was often criticized for prioritizing performance over performer wellbeing. Today's approach, while still demanding, generally includes more consideration for sustainable promotion schedules.

Creative Involvement

Yuna actively participated in shaping the album's creative direction, a level of involvement that varies among K-pop artists depending on their agency's policies and individual interests. Her input helped ensure that "Ice Cream" authentically represents her artistic vision rather than simply being content created for her.

This creative participation has become increasingly important to K-pop fans, who value authenticity and personal expression from their favorite artists. Idols who write, compose, or direct their own work often earn additional respect from discerning audiences.

Hopes For The Album

When asked about her aspirations for "Ice Cream," Yuna's answer was characteristically sweet. "My wish is for 'Ice Cream' to be an album that makes people smile," she said. "Even when you feel down after not sleeping well or when the weather isn't nice, I hope thinking of 'Ice Cream' can make you smile."

It's a modest goal in an industry often obsessed with chart positions and sales figures, but it speaks to the emotional connection that the best K-pop music creates with its listeners. Whether "Ice Cream" achieves commercial success remains to be seen, but Yuna's intentions are clear.

With ITZY riding high on viral attention and the infrastructure of JYP Entertainment behind her, Yuna has every advantage as she begins her solo journey. The coming weeks will reveal whether she can convert this favorable moment into lasting solo success.

Yuna's solo debut EP "Ice Cream" is available now on all major streaming platforms.
