IVE's Rei Openly Expresses She Felt Hurt After Fans Criticized Pricey Merch

March 17, 2026 By Andrew Lewter

In a rare display of vulnerability that has resonated deeply with fans, IVE's Rei became visibly emotional during a recent fan communication event while discussing how criticism over expensive merchandise has affected her personally. The moment has sparked widespread sympathy alongside renewed debate about the economics of K-Pop fandom and who bears responsibility for pricing decisions.

The emotional moment occurred during a Phoning session—IVE's official fan communication platform—where Rei was responding to questions from DIVEs (IVE's fanbase). When one fan mentioned struggling to afford recent merchandise releases, Rei's demeanor shifted noticeably, and she began addressing the topic with unexpected candor.

"I see what people say online," Rei said, her voice wavering. "They say we're greedy, that we don't care about fans who can't afford things. But we don't decide the prices. We don't even know what things will cost until they're announced, like everyone else. It hurts when people are angry at us for something we can't control."

The Merchandise Controversy

Rei's comments come amid growing discontent among IVE fans about the escalating cost of official merchandise. Recent releases, including a limited edition photo book priced at $150 and concert goods with individual items exceeding $50, have sparked accusations that Starship Entertainment is exploiting fans' dedication.

Comparison posts showing IVE merchandise prices alongside those of other groups have circulated widely, with critics arguing that the premium charged exceeds what the products' quality justifies. Particularly contentious was a recent collaboration merchandise line that some fans described as "mall quality at luxury prices."

"I've been a DIVE since debut. I want to support the girls, but I literally cannot afford to anymore. A photocard shouldn't cost as much as groceries for a week. When did this become normal?" — Fan post that received over 30K likes

The criticism has created a difficult environment for the IVE members, who are often the visible faces of merchandise announcements even though they have no actual role in pricing or product decisions. Fans direct their frustration at the members through comments and social media, despite the decisions being made entirely by company management.

Rei's Full Statement

During the Phoning session, Rei spent several minutes addressing the issue in what fans have described as one of the most honest idol statements about the business side of K-Pop in recent memory. While becoming emotional at points, she maintained composure enough to express her thoughts clearly.

"I understand that fans work hard for money. I really do. Before I debuted, I wasn't rich either. My family isn't wealthy. I know what it's like to want something and not be able to have it because of money. When fans say they feel pressured or sad because they can't buy things, I feel that too."

She continued: "But when the anger comes toward us—toward me and my members—it's confusing. We pose for the photos, we do the promotions, but we don't make the business decisions. I wish I could make everything affordable for everyone. But I can't. I don't have that power. I'm just an idol."

The statement concluded with Rei expressing hope that fans would continue supporting IVE through streaming and attendance at free events, emphasizing that financial contribution isn't the only form of valid fandom. "Your love means more than your money. I hope you know that."

Fan Response

The clip of Rei's emotional moment spread rapidly across social media, eliciting a wave of supportive responses. Many fans expressed guilt for directing criticism at idols rather than company management, acknowledging the unfairness of the situation.

"Watching Rei almost cry because of things we said made me feel terrible," one fan wrote. "She's right—we were blaming the wrong people. The members probably feel as trapped by this as we do." Similar sentiments were echoed thousands of times across platforms.

Fan communities have since pivoted their criticism more specifically toward Starship Entertainment, with organized campaigns calling for transparency in merchandise pricing and more affordable options for younger or less financially secure fans. Petitions requesting the company respond to fan concerns have gathered significant signatures.

"Rei reminded us that idols are workers, not owners. Starship makes the money decisions. Let's redirect our energy to where it belongs. The girls deserve better than to be caught in the middle." — Statement from major DIVE fan coalition

However, not all responses have been sympathetic. Some fans argue that idols benefit financially from merchandise sales through profit-sharing arrangements and therefore cannot claim complete innocence. Others suggest that Rei's statement, while sincere, was naive about the business realities of K-Pop and her role within them.

Starship Entertainment's Response

Starship Entertainment released a brief statement acknowledging fan feedback about merchandise pricing without directly addressing Rei's comments. The statement read: "We appreciate and carefully consider all feedback from fans. We strive to provide merchandise that reflects the quality and value fans expect while managing production and operational costs."

The statement was criticized by many as corporate boilerplate that failed to address specific concerns. No mention was made of potential price adjustments, expanded affordable options, or any concrete changes in response to fan discontent. The company also did not address how merchandise criticism affects their artists' wellbeing.

Industry observers note that meaningful pricing reform is unlikely given the proven willingness of fans to pay premium prices despite complaints. "K-Pop merchandise operates on scarcity economics," one analyst explained. "As long as enough fans pay the high prices, there's no business incentive to lower them. Complaints from those who can't afford it don't affect the bottom line."

The Broader Industry Issue

Rei's situation highlights a systemic issue across the K-Pop industry where idols become the face of decisions they don't make. Members announce comebacks they didn't schedule, promote products they didn't price, and participate in activities they may not have chosen—all while receiving the direct brunt of any resulting criticism.

This dynamic places idols in an impossible position: expected to maintain positive public images while serving as representatives for corporate decisions that may be unpopular. When things go wrong, companies can remain in the background while artists absorb the negative response.

Mental health professionals have raised concerns about this arrangement, noting the psychological strain of being held responsible for things beyond one's control. "Learned helplessness develops when people repeatedly experience situations where their actions have no effect on outcomes," one psychologist explained. "Idols who face criticism for company decisions are in exactly this position."

International Fan Perspectives

International DIVEs have been particularly vocal in the merchandise pricing debate, noting that shipping costs and currency conversion often double the effective price of goods for fans outside Korea. A photobook that Korean fans consider expensive becomes prohibitively costly for international supporters.

This has led to calls for regional pricing, local distribution partnerships, and digital alternatives that don't require physical shipping. While some K-Pop companies have taken steps in this direction, implementation remains inconsistent, and IVE's international merchandise accessibility has been criticized as below industry standards.

The situation creates a two-tiered fandom where Korean fans have significantly greater access to official goods, concert attendance, and fan events, while international supporters—often the majority of a group's fanbase—must accept higher costs and less access as the price of geographic distance.

Moving Forward

Whether Rei's emotional appeal will result in any concrete changes remains to be seen. History suggests that K-Pop companies are resistant to fan pressure on pricing issues, particularly when sales figures remain strong regardless of complaints.

For Rei personally, the incident has strengthened her connection with many fans who appreciated her honesty, even as it may have complicated her relationship with company management by drawing attention to a sensitive business issue. Sources close to the group suggest she has not faced any negative consequences for her comments, though the true internal response may never be publicly known.

What's certain is that the conversation Rei inadvertently sparked extends far beyond IVE or any single group. The economics of K-Pop fandom—who profits, who pays, and who gets blamed when the system creates discontent—is a fundamental tension that the industry has yet to meaningfully address.

For now, fans must navigate the uncomfortable reality that supporting their favorite artists often means enriching corporations whose interests may not align with either fan or artist welfare. Rei's tears reminded everyone of the human cost of these structural problems—even if solutions remain frustratingly out of reach.
