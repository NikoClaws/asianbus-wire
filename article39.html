Seoul's Red Lights For BTS Spark Political Firestorm: HYBE Denies PPP Connection

March 19, 2026 By Brandon Lee

What should have been a celebration of BTS's historic comeback has descended into political controversy after Seoul's decision to bathe major landmarks in red lighting ahead of the group's Gwanghwamun Square concert. The color choice has drawn accusations that the city is using the event to signal support for the conservative People Power Party (PPP), whose official color is red—forcing HYBE to issue an unprecedented statement clarifying that the color represents the album's artistic concept, not political allegiance.

The controversy erupted earlier this week when photographs emerged showing Namsan Tower, several bridges over the Han River, and buildings around Gwanghwamun Square illuminated in vivid red. While such lighting installations are common for major events, the timing—amid South Korea's deeply polarized political climate—immediately raised eyebrows. Critics pointed out that red is associated with the conservative PPP, while the progressive Democratic Party of Korea uses blue.

BTS's fandom ARMY, known for their signature purple color ("I purple you" being a phrase coined by member V), quickly mobilized in opposition. The hashtag #BTSisPurple trended globally within hours, with fans expressing dismay that their artists were being dragged into political discourse.

HYBE's Official Response

On Wednesday, HYBE issued a formal statement addressing the growing controversy. The company emphasized that red is the designated color theme for the "ARIRANG" album and that they specifically requested the city use this color for the promotional lighting.

"The color red was chosen as the key visual element of 'ARIRANG' to represent passion, the Korean spirit, and the emotional intensity of reunion. We specifically requested that Seoul use this color for promotional purposes. It has no political meaning whatsoever. We ask that people do not overinterpret this artistic choice." — HYBE Official Statement

The company's rare direct address of a political matter reflects the seriousness with which they view the controversy. HYBE typically avoids commenting on anything that could be perceived as political, making this statement all the more notable.

The Political Context

To understand why lighting color became national news, one must consider South Korea's current political environment. The country has been deeply divided since the impeachment of former President Yoon Suk-yeol and the subsequent election of progressive President Lee Jae-myung. Tensions between conservative and progressive camps remain high, and symbolic gestures are scrutinized intensely.

Red, as the PPP's official color, carries unmistakable political connotations for many Koreans. When major Seoul landmarks suddenly turned red days before a concert featuring the country's most famous cultural ambassadors, some viewed it as a provocative political statement—particularly given reports that President Lee personally endorsed the Gwanghwamun concert.

Conservative commentators have accused the Lee administration of exploiting BTS's comeback for political purposes, while progressive voices counter that the criticism is manufactured outrage designed to create division around an apolitical cultural event.

ARMY's Response And #BTSisPurple

BTS's fandom has historically prided itself on being apolitical as a community, focusing instead on the group's message of self-love and youth empowerment. The politicization of the comeback has frustrated many fans who simply want to celebrate the music.

The #BTSisPurple campaign represents an attempt to reclaim the narrative. Fans have flooded social media with purple imagery, emphasizing that purple—not red or blue—is the color that represents BTS and ARMY's bond. Many have shared clips of BTS members specifically stating their intention to remain neutral on political matters.

"We've spent years building a community based on positivity and inclusivity," wrote one popular fan account. "Now people are trying to turn our artists into political symbols. BTS belongs to ARMY, not to any party."

However, some fans have noted that the anger should be directed at those politicizing the event rather than at HYBE or the city of Seoul. "Red is just a color," argued one commenter. "The album art uses red. The teasers use red. Making this political says more about the accusers than about BTS."

Presidential Involvement Adds Fuel

Complicating matters further, President Lee Jae-myung publicly addressed the concert this week, commenting on the security preparations and emphasizing the importance of the event for Korea's global cultural standing. While his remarks were focused on logistics and safety, the fact that a sitting president commented on a K-Pop concert was itself unusual.

Critics immediately seized on the presidential attention as evidence of politicization. "When has a president ever commented on a K-Pop concert?" asked one opposition lawmaker. "This is clearly an attempt to associate the administration with BTS's popularity."

Supporters countered that an event expecting 260,000 attendees naturally warrants presidential awareness and that previous administrations have similarly acknowledged major cultural events.

Bang Si-hyuk's Role Questioned

Adding another layer to the controversy, reports emerged that HYBE chairman Bang Si-hyuk personally proposed using Gwanghwamun Square as the concert venue. While this has been presented by some as evidence of corporate-government coordination, others note that the location's symbolic importance made it a logical choice for such a historic event.

Bang Si-hyuk has not publicly commented on the political controversy. HYBE's official position remains that all decisions were made based on artistic and logistical considerations rather than political motivations.

International Media Coverage

International outlets have covered the controversy with varying degrees of context. Some have framed it as an example of South Korea's hyper-partisan political environment, while others have focused on the unusual intersection of K-Pop and politics. The Guardian's coverage noted that "even cultural events cannot escape the gravitational pull of Korean political division."

For BTS, who have spoken at the United Nations and met with multiple world leaders, navigating political sensitivity is not new. However, facing accusations of partisan alignment within their home country represents a different kind of challenge—one that could affect their carefully maintained universal appeal.

The Bigger Picture

Beyond the immediate controversy, the situation raises questions about the intersection of culture and politics in modern Korea. Can major cultural events exist outside political interpretation? In a society where every color, location, and symbol carries potential political meaning, how should artists and companies navigate public perception?

For now, BTS and HYBE appear committed to maintaining their apolitical stance while the concert proceeds as planned. Whether the red lighting controversy will be remembered as a minor pre-concert distraction or as a more significant moment in the politicization of K-Pop remains to be seen.

As one longtime industry observer noted: "The irony is that by making this a political issue, the critics are achieving exactly what they claim to oppose—making BTS's comeback about politics rather than music."
