SEVENTEEN Hit With Album Sales Fraud Allegations As HYBE Court Documents Surface

March 18, 2026 By Michelle Lee 📖 8 min read

SEVENTEEN has become the latest target in the ongoing saga surrounding HYBE Entertainment's business practices, with viral social media posts alleging that the group engaged in "sajaegi"—the Korean term for artificially inflating album sales. The accusations emerged after court documents from HYBE's legal battle with former ADOR CEO Min Hee Jin revealed that approximately 500,000 albums were returned out of 650,000 reported sales for an unnamed HYBE group, leading some internet users to point fingers at SEVENTEEN, who hold records for the best-selling K-pop albums of all time.

However, fans of the thirteen-member group have fiercely defended their artists, presenting counter-evidence that they say exonerates SEVENTEEN from the accusations. The controversy has reignited broader debates about transparency in K-pop album sales, the ethics of bulk purchasing, and the ongoing civil war between different factions of HYBE's fandoms.

The situation exemplifies the increasingly toxic environment surrounding K-pop discourse, where allegations can spread virally regardless of their factual basis, and where fans find themselves constantly on the defensive against attacks from rival fandoms and general critics alike.

The Court Documents That Started It All

The controversy traces back to court documents that emerged from HYBE's ongoing legal dispute with Min Hee Jin, the former CEO of ADOR (the subsidiary managing NewJeans). Within these documents, claims were made that HYBE artificially inflated album sales for some of its groups, with one specific allegation noting that around 500,000 albums were returned after 650,000 were initially reported sold for an unnamed group.

This practice, if true, would constitute what Koreans call "sajaegi"—essentially manipulating sales figures by having companies or affiliated parties purchase large quantities of albums, report them as sales, and then quietly return them after charting periods end. Such practices are technically illegal under Korean fair trade laws and are considered deeply unethical within the music industry.

Importantly, the court documents did not name SEVENTEEN or any specific group. However, some social media users, apparently motivated by inter-fandom rivalry, began spreading allegations that SEVENTEEN was the unnamed group—claims that quickly went viral despite lacking direct evidence.

Why SEVENTEEN Was Targeted

SEVENTEEN's position as one of K-pop's most commercially successful groups made them an obvious target for those looking to attach names to the unnamed allegations. The group's 2023 album "FML" currently holds the record as the best-selling K-pop album of all time, while "Seventeenth Heaven" sits in second place. These extraordinary sales figures have long attracted both admiration and skepticism.

Critics of the group seized on photos that surfaced showing what appeared to be large quantities of SEVENTEEN's "17 Is Right Here" album being discarded or set aside for recycling. These images were presented as "proof" that albums were being purchased in bulk for the inclusions (photocards and other collectibles) and then thrown away, with some implying this was evidence of company-orchestrated sajaegi.

"Court documents show that HYBE returned 500K albums out of 650K sales figure for one of their groups." — Viral tweet spreading the allegations

The timing of the allegations—amid HYBE's legal troubles and the broader "HYBE civil war" that has divided K-pop fans—likely contributed to their rapid spread. Anti-HYBE sentiment has been running high following various controversies, making any negative news about HYBE-affiliated groups susceptible to amplification.

Fans Mount Vigorous Defense

CARATs, as SEVENTEEN fans are known, quickly mobilized to defend their group, presenting multiple arguments against the allegations. Their defense centered on several key points that they say debunk the connection between SEVENTEEN and the court document claims.

First, fans pointed out a crucial numerical discrepancy. The court documents reference an album with 650,000 in sales figures—but SEVENTEEN hasn't had an album selling that relatively modest amount since 2018, before HYBE's acquisition of Pledis Entertainment (SEVENTEEN's agency). Their recent albums have sold millions of copies, making the 650,000 figure inconsistent with SEVENTEEN's recent releases.

"Out of the 650k sales figure—the last time SEVENTEEN had an album that sold that much was in 2018, pre-acquisition," wrote one fan. "If you're going to drag, at least be intelligent about it."

Second, fans addressed the photos of discarded albums, explaining a practice that's unfortunately common throughout K-pop but unrelated to company sajaegi. Many fans purchase multiple album copies specifically to obtain random photocards and other collectibles, keeping the inclusions while discarding the actual albums. While wasteful, this fan-driven behavior is distinct from company-orchestrated sales manipulation.

"The boxes are empty, so is your head," one fan pointedly responded to the allegations, noting that the discarded items in the viral photos were empty album packaging from which inclusions had already been removed—evidence of fan collector behavior rather than returned company stock.

The Broader Sajaegi Problem

While fans have mounted a strong defense of SEVENTEEN specifically, the controversy has brought renewed attention to longstanding concerns about transparency in K-pop album sales. The industry's reliance on album sales as a measure of success, combined with the importance of first-week numbers for charting and prestige, creates incentives for manipulation.

Various forms of potential sales inflation exist, from outright company purchases to more gray-area practices like bundling albums with concert tickets or offering multiple versions with exclusive inclusions that encourage fans to buy multiple copies. The line between legitimate marketing and manipulation is not always clear, and different fans and critics draw that line in different places.

HYBE has faced sajaegi allegations before, though the company has consistently denied engaging in such practices. The court documents' emergence has added fuel to these pre-existing suspicions, even if the specific connection to SEVENTEEN remains unproven.

Inter-Fandom Warfare

A significant factor in the spread of the allegations appears to be the toxic inter-fandom dynamics that have increasingly characterized K-pop discussions online. The accusations against SEVENTEEN emerged from and spread through accounts associated with rival fandoms, suggesting that competitive animosity rather than genuine concern about industry practices may be driving the controversy.

The "HYBE civil war"—the ongoing conflict between fans of different HYBE groups, as well as between HYBE supporters and critics—has created an environment where negative information about any HYBE group is quickly weaponized. SEVENTEEN, despite being under Pledis rather than directly under HYBE, gets caught up in these conflicts due to Pledis's subsidiary status.

"It's exhausting," wrote one CARAT on a fan forum. "Every time there's any controversy involving HYBE, people try to drag SEVENTEEN into it. We just want to support our artists in peace, but we're constantly having to defend against bad-faith attacks."

Industry Response And Implications

Neither SEVENTEEN, Pledis Entertainment, nor HYBE have officially responded to the specific allegations, following the common industry practice of not dignifying unsubstantiated social media accusations with official statements. This silence, while strategically understandable, has frustrated both fans who want vindication and critics who interpret it as tacit admission.

The controversy does highlight the need for greater transparency in K-pop sales reporting. Currently, album sales figures come from various tracking services with different methodologies, and the behind-the-scenes mechanics of how those numbers are generated remain opaque to fans and outside observers.

Industry analysts have suggested that implementing more rigorous verification systems could help protect artists from unfair accusations while also deterring any actual manipulation. However, such changes would require buy-in from companies, distributors, and tracking services—a coordination challenge that has so far proven difficult to achieve.

The Human Cost Of Viral Allegations

Beyond the corporate and industry implications, the controversy takes a toll on fans who feel they must constantly defend artists they love from attacks they perceive as unfair. The emotional labor of monitoring social media for false claims, compiling evidence, and engaging in endless arguments can be exhausting and demoralizing.

It also raises questions about the artists themselves. While SEVENTEEN members have not publicly addressed the allegations, they are undoubtedly aware of them, and the experience of being falsely accused—even indirectly—cannot be pleasant. The parasocial nature of K-pop means that attacks on groups feel personal to both artists and fans.

As one fan reflected: "These are real people being accused of fraud based on literally no evidence. Even if the allegations don't stick, they create a cloud of suspicion that never fully goes away. It's unfair to the members who work so hard."
