BTS Achieves Rare Billboard Sweep — ARIRANG No. 1 On Billboard 200, 'SWIM' Tops Hot 100

April 1, 2026 10 min read By Daniel Cho

BTS has pulled off a rare Billboard double crown, with their comeback album ARIRANG debuting at No. 1 on the Billboard 200 and lead single "SWIM" simultaneously topping the Hot 100 — making it the biggest chart week of 2026 by any artist, and one of the most dominant debut weeks in Billboard history. The album moved a staggering 641,000 equivalent album units in its first week, proving once and for all that the four-year military hiatus did nothing to diminish BTS's commercial power.

Let's put those numbers in perspective. 641,000 units. Of those, roughly 532,000 came from pure sales — the kind of number that the music industry assumed was extinct in the streaming age. And within those sales, 208,000 were vinyl purchases alone. Yes, vinyl. In 2026. BTS sold more vinyl copies in one week than most artists sell total albums in a year.

The figure marks the largest first-week performance since Taylor Swift's The Life of a Showgirl last fall, and the biggest sales week for a group since One Direction's Midnight Memories way back in 2013. But even those comparisons feel insufficient. This is a Korean-language album, named after a folk song, released by a group that hasn't put out new material together in nearly four years. By every conventional metric, ARIRANG shouldn't have done this. And yet.

Seventh Hot 100 No. 1: Joining The Beatles

"SWIM" didn't just top the Hot 100. It topped it convincingly, logging 15.3 million streams, 25.8 million radio impressions, and 154,000 combined sales for the tracking week. Those are first-week numbers that most artists would kill for on their biggest single, let alone a track from an album that's competing with itself across the entire chart.

The achievement makes BTS the fifth act in history with seven or more Hot 100 chart-toppers, joining The Beatles, The Supremes, Bee Gees, and The Rolling Stones. Read that list again. Those are the most iconic names in the history of popular music. BTS — seven guys from South Korea who debuted on a shoestring budget with a small company that was nearly bankrupt — now stands alongside them in the record books.

It's also the group's first Hot 100 No. 1 since 2021's "Butter," though members Jimin and Jung Kook each scored solo No. 1 hits during BTS's hiatus. Jimin's "Like Crazy" and Jung Kook's "Seven" proved that individual members could carry the Hot 100 on their own, but there's something different about the group doing it together. It feels bigger. It is bigger.

"When they debuted with 'No More Dream' in 2013, nobody — and I mean nobody — predicted this trajectory," recalled one Korean music industry veteran who has followed BTS since their pre-debut days. "They were from a tiny company. They didn't have the Big Three backing. And now they're in the same sentence as The Beatles. It's the most unlikely success story in pop music history."

ARIRANG Dominates The Entire Hot 100

The album's impact on the Hot 100 extends far beyond its lead single. An astonishing 13 of ARIRANG's 14 tracks entered the chart simultaneously — a feat that puts the album in elite company alongside only a handful of releases in Hot 100 history.

"Body to Body" charted at No. 25, followed by "Hooligan" at No. 35, "FYA" at No. 36, and "Normal" at No. 41. The album's remaining charting tracks filled positions throughout the back half of the chart, demonstrating genuine listener engagement across the entire tracklist rather than concentrated interest in a single hit.

The only track that missed the Hot 100 was "No. 29," a 98-second instrumental featuring the historic Seongdeok Bell — a 1,300-year-old Korean artifact whose deep, resonant tone serves as a sonic palette cleanser between the album's two halves. It's the kind of artistic choice that a commercially-focused act would never make, and the fact that BTS included it anyway speaks to the creative confidence that permeates ARIRANG.

The Hot 100 domination also puts to rest a persistent criticism that BTS's chart success has been primarily driven by organized fan purchasing rather than genuine listenership. You don't get 13 songs on the Hot 100 simultaneously through bulk buying alone. That requires real, broad-based streaming engagement — the kind that comes from an album that people actually want to listen to, start to finish.

The Vinyl Phenomenon

Perhaps the most surprising number in ARIRANG's first-week haul is the vinyl figure: 208,000 copies. To understand how absurd this is, consider that the bestselling vinyl album of 2025 — Taylor Swift's The Life of a Showgirl — moved approximately 190,000 vinyl units in its first week. BTS just beat that, making ARIRANG the biggest vinyl debut in modern chart history.

The vinyl push was, admittedly, by design. HYBE released ARIRANG in multiple vinyl variants — including a limited-edition pressing on translucent blue wax, a picture disc featuring the album's traditional Korean artwork, and a standard black pressing — alongside CD versions and a cassette tape edition. K-pop's physical sales culture, where fans purchase multiple versions for different photocards and inclusions, clearly played a role.

But the vinyl numbers also reflect something deeper: a desire among fans to own ARIRANG in a format that feels permanent and tangible. After waiting nearly four years for a group album, ARMY wasn't satisfied with streams alone. They wanted something they could hold, display, and keep. The vinyl sales are, in a way, a love letter back to the group — a physical manifestation of "we're not going anywhere either."

The Comeback That Silenced The Doubters

Let's be honest: there were doubters. During BTS's nearly four-year hiatus, the K-pop landscape shifted dramatically. Stray Kids scored seven consecutive Billboard 200 No. 1 albums. SEVENTEEN sold out stadiums worldwide. NewJeans became a cultural phenomenon. LE SSERAFIM broke streaming records. Fourth-generation groups were ascendant, and the conventional wisdom in some corners of the industry was that BTS's window had closed.

The doubters had a plausible case. Extended hiatuses have derailed plenty of musical careers before. Tastes change. Audiences move on. The artists themselves evolve in ways that don't always align with what made them successful in the first place. It would have been perfectly reasonable for BTS to come back to strong-but-diminished numbers — a respectable showing that acknowledged the passage of time.

Instead, they came back and posted the biggest first week of 2026. Not the biggest K-pop first week. The biggest first week, period. By anyone. In any genre.

"I think what people underestimated was the emotional weight of this comeback," said Billboard chart analyst Keith Caulfield. "This isn't just a new album. For millions of fans, this represents the end of a waiting period that coincided with some of the most difficult years of their lives. The pandemic, the isolation, the uncertainty — and then their favorite group goes away for military service on top of all that. When BTS came back, people weren't just buying an album. They were celebrating a reunion."

RM's Quiet Gratitude

In typical fashion, BTS's response to the Billboard news was understated. Leader RM shared the chart results on Instagram with a characteristically brief caption: "Sending my love and thanks." No celebration selfie. No champagne. Just five words that somehow conveyed more gratitude than a thousand-word press statement could.

Through Big Hit Music, the group added a formal statement: "We hope this song has provided even a small measure of comfort and courage to listeners across borders." The statement didn't mention records, chart positions, or sales figures. It talked about comfort. About courage. About crossing borders.

It's the kind of message that explains why ARMY's loyalty has endured through years of separation. BTS has always treated their success as something that belongs to their fans as much as to themselves. The No. 1 debut isn't framed as "we did it" but as "we hope this helped you." In an industry that often runs on ego, it's a refreshing posture — and after 13 years, it still feels genuine rather than calculated.

J-Hope was slightly less restrained, posting a series of Instagram stories that showed him dancing around his apartment to "SWIM" while his dog watched with visible confusion. Jungkook posted a blurry late-night selfie with the caption "7." V, predictably, posted a photo of a cloud that bore no obvious relation to anything.

The World Tour Looms Large

BTS will kick off their world tour April 9–12 at Goyang Stadium in Gyeonggi Province, South Korea, with 82 concerts planned across approximately 34 cities worldwide. The tour — dubbed "BTS TOUR: THE RETURN" — is the group's largest ever, spanning six continents over nearly a year.

Given the first-week album numbers, ticket demand for the tour is expected to be stratospheric. Many dates have already sold out, with resale prices for opening-weekend shows in Seoul reaching five figures. The tour's North American leg, which includes multiple nights at SoFi Stadium in Los Angeles and MetLife Stadium in New Jersey, is expected to generate some of the highest-grossing concert figures in 2026.

The tour also represents a logistical milestone for the live entertainment industry. With 82 dates across 34 cities, the production team is managing one of the most complex touring operations in concert history. The stage design, which reportedly incorporates elements inspired by traditional Korean architecture, requires over 100 trucks of equipment and a crew of nearly 500 people.

Where ARIRANG Fits In K-Pop History

The K-pop landscape shifted significantly during BTS's absence. Stray Kids alone scored seven Billboard 200 No. 1s in that time, proving that the market BTS helped build could sustain other acts at the highest level. SEVENTEEN, ENHYPEN, and fourth-generation groups filled stadiums and topped charts worldwide. The ecosystem was thriving without BTS, which is arguably BTS's greatest legacy: they didn't just succeed in the global market, they opened it for everyone.

But ARIRANG's record-breaking debut proves something else: that the group's grip on the global charts hasn't loosened one bit. If anything, the hiatus served to compress the demand, creating a dam of anticipation that the album's release finally broke. The result is a flood of numbers that dwarfs everything else in 2026.

Where does this leave BTS in the historical conversation? The Beatles comparison, once considered hyperbolic, is looking increasingly apt. Both acts shattered cultural barriers — the Beatles for British rock, BTS for Korean pop. Both maintained an extraordinarily devoted fanbase that outlasted cultural shifts and industry skepticism. Both produced music that evolved dramatically over time while maintaining a core emotional authenticity. And now both hold seven or more Hot 100 No. 1s.

Whether ARIRANG represents BTS's peak or merely the beginning of their next act remains to be seen. But with a world tour about to launch, a Netflix documentary premiering this week, and chart positions that most artists would sacrifice limbs for, the ARIRANG era is already one for the history books.

BTS's world tour begins April 9 at Goyang Stadium. "BTS: The Return" documentary is now streaming on Netflix. ARIRANG is available on all major streaming platforms.
