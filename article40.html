THE BOYZ File For Contract Termination: Explosive Allegations Against ONE HUNDRED

March 19, 2026 By Selena Jung

In a bombshell development that has sent shockwaves through the K-Pop industry, THE BOYZ has filed for contract termination against their agency ONE HUNDRED Entertainment (formerly IST Entertainment), citing alleged breach of trust, unpaid compensation, and misuse of dormitory funds. The filing, reported by K-media outlet The Fact on March 19, marks one of the most dramatic artist-agency disputes in recent memory and could reshape the future of one of K-Pop's most beloved third-generation groups.

The 11-member group, which debuted in 2017 and rose to prominence through their "Road to Kingdom" victory, has reportedly been in escalating conflict with their agency for months. The contract termination filing represents the culmination of grievances that members and their legal representatives claim constitute grounds for voiding their exclusive contracts.

The Allegations

According to the filing, THE BOYZ's legal team has outlined three primary categories of alleged contract violations:

1. Unpaid Compensation: The filing alleges that members have not received full payment for various activities including concert revenue, merchandise sales, and overseas promotions. Sources close to the situation claim the disputed amount could be in the billions of won. The members reportedly requested detailed financial accounting multiple times but received incomplete or delayed responses.

2. Dorm Fund Misuse: Perhaps the most shocking allegation involves claims that funds designated for member dormitory expenses were redirected to other company purposes. The filing reportedly includes documentary evidence showing discrepancies between amounts deducted from member earnings for housing and actual expenditures on their living quarters.

3. Breach of Trust: Beyond financial matters, the legal filing cites broader breaches of the trust relationship between artist and agency. These allegedly include inadequate communication about career decisions, failure to consult members on schedules and activities, and actions that prioritized company interests over artist welfare.

"After years of attempting to resolve these issues through internal dialogue, we have concluded that formal legal action is necessary to protect our clients' rights and interests." — Statement from THE BOYZ legal representation

ONE HUNDRED's Response

ONE HUNDRED Entertainment wasted no time in rejecting the termination request. In a statement released hours after the filing became public, the agency denied all allegations and characterized the legal action as "baseless and without merit."

"ONE HUNDRED Entertainment categorically denies the allegations raised in the contract termination filing," the company stated. "We have always operated with transparency and in accordance with our contractual obligations. We will take all necessary legal measures to protect the company's rights and reputation."

The agency further claimed that all financial settlements have been conducted according to contract terms and that members have always had access to relevant documentation. Regarding the dormitory fund allegations specifically, ONE HUNDRED stated that all housing-related expenses were properly documented and used for their intended purposes.

Background: THE BOYZ And Agency Tensions

THE BOYZ debuted under Cre.ker Entertainment in 2017 before the agency was absorbed into IST Entertainment in 2021, which later rebranded to ONE HUNDRED Entertainment. The group achieved significant success, winning Mnet's "Road to Kingdom" competition in 2020 and building a devoted international fanbase called "The B."

However, fans have long speculated about potential tensions between the group and management. Social media discussions have noted perceived decreases in promotional support, limited solo activities for members, and what some fans described as "declining production quality" in recent releases.

The transition through multiple agency reorganizations may have contributed to the current conflict. Changes in management, corporate culture, and business priorities during the IST to ONE HUNDRED transition could have created conditions for the disputes now surfacing.

Industry Implications

The filing comes at a time of heightened scrutiny of artist-agency relationships in K-Pop. Recent years have seen multiple high-profile contract disputes, with artists increasingly willing to challenge agency practices through legal channels. The outcomes of these cases have varied, with some artists successfully terminating contracts and others remaining bound to their original agreements.

Legal experts note that Korean courts have become more receptive to artist claims in recent years, particularly when financial impropriety or breach of trust can be demonstrated. However, proving such allegations typically requires substantial documentation, and agencies often possess superior legal resources.

"Contract termination cases in K-Pop are always complex," noted entertainment lawyer Kim Sung-ho. "The burden of proof rests with the artists, but courts have shown willingness to side with performers when evidence of contract violations is compelling. The dormitory fund allegations, if documented, could be particularly damaging to the agency's position."

Fan Reaction: #FreeTheBOYZ

The B community has rallied around the group with the hashtag #FreeTheBOYZ trending globally within hours of the news breaking. Fan sentiment overwhelmingly supports the members, with many expressing that they had long suspected mistreatment.

"We've watched THE BOYZ struggle for years while the company ignored them," wrote one fan. "They deserve so much better. Whatever they need to do to protect themselves, we support them."

Others have compiled timelines of perceived agency neglect, pointing to limited comebacks, sparse individual activities, and what fans describe as inadequate promotion compared to the group's popularity and talent level.

However, some voices in the fan community have urged caution, noting that only one side of the story is currently public and that legal disputes often involve complex circumstances not immediately apparent to outside observers.

What Happens Next?

The contract termination process in Korea typically proceeds through several stages. ONE HUNDRED's rejection of the termination request means the matter will likely proceed to formal arbitration or court proceedings unless a settlement is reached.

During this period, THE BOYZ members remain technically bound by their contracts, though the legal uncertainty may effectively pause their activities. Historically, agencies have sometimes ceased promoting artists during contract disputes, viewing continued investment as risky.

If THE BOYZ ultimately prevails, members would be free to sign with other agencies or potentially form their own management company. If ONE HUNDRED's position is upheld, members could be required to continue fulfilling their contracts, possibly with additional obligations.

The case could take months or even years to fully resolve, during which time one of K-Pop's most talented groups faces an uncertain future.
