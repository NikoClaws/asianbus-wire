"Truly Terrifying" — Former Cube Entertainment Idols Expose The Harsh Trainee Culture

March 23, 2026 10 min read By Julian Choi

Three veteran K-pop idols have pulled back the curtain on the brutal reality of trainee life at one of Korea's major entertainment agencies. BTOB's Lee Changsub, Apink's Park Chorong, and HIGHLIGHT's Son Dongwoon gathered for a candid conversation that has left fans both shocked and deeply concerned about the industry's treatment of aspiring artists.

The revelations came during a video titled "Showing Current K-Pop How It's Done," released on Changsub's YouTube channel. What began as a lighthearted reunion between former Cube Entertainment trainees quickly evolved into a sobering discussion about the psychological and physical pressures they endured during their pre-debut years.

The Weekly Evaluation System

The production team surprised Changsub at the beginning of the video by announcing, "Didn't you used to work at Cube? We've gathered former Cube employees." When Chorong and Dongwoon appeared as guests, the three exchanged warm greetings before diving into memories that were anything but warm.

Recalling her trainee days, Chorong started with the evaluation system that governed their lives. "There were evaluations every weekend and at the end of each month," she explained, her voice carrying the weight of those memories.

Changsub added crucial context about what these evaluations meant for young trainees' futures. "Weekly evaluations were just about what you practiced that week," he clarified, "but the monthly evaluations decided whether you stayed or got cut."

The tension in Chorong's response was palpable: "It was truly terrifying."

The Constant Fear of Being Cut

Changsub elaborated on the psychological toll of this system, painting a picture of perpetual anxiety. "Every month was nerve-wracking," he revealed. "They cut people without hesitation."

But it wasn't just performance that determined a trainee's fate. Weight became a crucial factor in whether young artists would see their dreams realized or crushed. "If you couldn't lose weight, you got cut," Changsub stated bluntly, a revelation that visibly surprised even the production team.

The strict weight monitoring began immediately upon arrival at the company. When Chorong mentioned, "Every Monday we had to weigh ourselves," Changsub nodded in recognition. "We did the same," he confirmed, before sharing his own dramatic transformation: "I weighed 80kg when I joined but lost down to 60kg."

That's a loss of approximately 44 pounds—a significant and potentially dangerous amount of weight, especially for a young person still developing physically.

The Impossible Standards

Perhaps most disturbing were the specific weight targets that trainees were expected to meet. Changsub outlined the standards with clinical precision: "For women, they wanted weights in the low 40kg range, and for men, mid-60kg. Taller trainees were expected to be in the low 70kg range."

To put these numbers in perspective, the "low 40kg range" translates to roughly 88-99 pounds—a weight that would be considered underweight for most adult women by medical standards. The pressure to meet these targets, combined with the threat of being cut from the program, created an environment ripe for disordered eating and psychological harm.

Health experts have long criticized K-pop's weight culture, noting that such extreme standards can lead to eating disorders, nutritional deficiencies, and long-term health problems. Yet the practice persists across the industry, with agencies prioritizing visual aesthetics over trainee wellbeing.

Chorong's Harrowing Memory

The most emotionally affecting moment of the conversation came when Chorong shared a particularly painful memory from her trainee days. Struggling to meet the weight requirements, she received a warning that still haunts her.

"When I couldn't lose weight, the dance teacher said, 'Chorong, you're in danger now. There's talk coming from above,'" she recalled. The "talk from above" referred to company executives discussing her potential removal from the program.

Her response to that warning was heartbreaking: "I remember running on the treadmill crying after hearing that."

The image of a young woman, likely still a teenager, sobbing while exercising in desperate fear of losing her dream encapsulates the darker side of the K-pop industry that fans rarely see. It's a stark contrast to the polished, effortless performances these artists eventually deliver on stage.

Industry-Wide Problem

While this conversation focused specifically on Cube Entertainment, the issues raised are hardly unique to one agency. Former trainees and idols from across the K-pop industry have shared similar stories over the years, painting a consistent picture of an industry that prioritizes appearance over health.

SM Entertainment, JYP Entertainment, YG Entertainment, and virtually every major agency have faced criticism for their trainee practices. Some have made efforts to reform, implementing more humane policies and providing mental health support. But the fundamental pressure to meet extreme visual standards persists.

The revelations are particularly relevant as the K-pop industry continues to expand globally, attracting more young people than ever to audition for Korean agencies. Many of these aspiring idols are unaware of the physical and psychological demands they may face if accepted as trainees.

Fan Response

The video has generated significant discussion among K-pop fans, many of whom expressed concern about the treatment these beloved artists endured. Comments ranged from sympathy to anger at the industry practices.

"Knowing that Chorong cried on the treadmill while trying to keep her dream alive... it breaks my heart," one fan wrote. "These agencies need to be held accountable."

Others pointed out the irony of the video's title: "They're 'Showing Current K-Pop How It's Done,' but maybe they should also show current K-pop what NOT to do. These practices need to end."

Some fans defended the industry, arguing that the high standards are what produce world-class performers. "It's harsh, but it's what makes K-pop K-pop," one commenter argued, though this view was met with significant pushback.

The Survivors' Success

Despite the brutal conditions they endured, all three artists went on to successful careers. BTOB debuted in 2012 and became one of K-pop's most respected vocal groups. Apink, also debuting in 2011, established themselves as one of the most successful girl groups of their generation. HIGHLIGHT (formerly BEAST) has been active since 2009 and continues to release music and tour.

Their success, however, raises uncomfortable questions. How many talented individuals were cut from programs due to weight rather than ability? How many suffered lasting psychological or physical harm from the trainee system? How many dreams were crushed not because of lack of talent, but because of a number on a scale?

Calls for Reform

The conversation has renewed calls for industry reform, including more transparent practices, better mental health support for trainees, and the involvement of medical professionals in weight management decisions. Some advocates have called for government oversight of entertainment company trainee programs.

Korean entertainment agencies have historically resisted outside interference, viewing their trainee systems as proprietary methods for developing talent. But as K-pop's global influence grows, so does scrutiny of its practices. International fans and media have increasingly questioned whether the industry's demanding culture crosses ethical lines.

For now, the candid revelations from Changsub, Chorong, and Dongwoon serve as a reminder that behind every polished K-pop performance lies years of sacrifice—and, too often, suffering. Whether this awareness translates into meaningful change remains to be seen.
