BLACKPINK Jisoo's "Boyfriend On Demand" Receives Mixed Reviews: Acting Debut Divides Critics

March 24, 2026 10 min read By AsianBus News Staff

With "Boyfriend on Demand" now complete on Netflix, reviews of BLACKPINK Jisoo's performance as leading lady Han Gyeong-nam have painted a complicated picture. Critics acknowledge her undeniable screen presence and charm while questioning whether the K-pop superstar has yet to achieve the acting breakthrough that would establish her as a dramatic talent in her own right.

The 16-episode romantic comedy, which wrapped its run last week, has been one of Netflix's most-watched K-dramas of 2026—a commercial success driven in large part by Jisoo's massive global fanbase. But commercial performance and critical reception don't always align, and the reviews emerging as the dust settles reveal a nuanced, sometimes divided assessment.

"Boyfriend on Demand" casts Jisoo as Gyeong-nam, a successful career woman who, following a public breakup, hires an AI-powered robot boyfriend to maintain appearances. The premise combines romantic comedy conventions with light science fiction elements, requiring Jisoo to navigate both comedic timing and emotional vulnerability.

The Case For Jisoo

Supporters of Jisoo's performance point to several strengths that shouldn't be overlooked. Her natural screen presence—honed through years of variety show appearances and music video work—translates into an easy watchability that keeps viewers engaged even through weaker script moments.

"The leads nail the charm offensive," wrote one reviewer on MyDramaList, highlighting the chemistry between Jisoo and co-star Seo In-guk. "You believe they could fall for each other because both bring genuine warmth to their interactions."

Jisoo's comedic timing has also earned praise. The show's lighter moments—including several physical comedy sequences and deadpan reactions to absurd situations—play to her strengths as a performer comfortable with entertainment formats.

Additionally, some critics note improvement from her previous dramatic work in "Snowdrop" (2021-2022). While that earlier series was mired in historical controversy, Jisoo's performance was often cited as one of its stronger elements. "Boyfriend on Demand" suggests continued growth and increasing comfort with the medium.

The Critique

However, a recurring theme in critical assessments is the sense that Jisoo hasn't yet found her "breakthrough" moment—the performance that would silence doubters and establish her as a serious actress beyond her idol credentials.

"Jisoo's acting is serviceable; she just hasn't found that breakthrough to the next level yet," wrote one particularly nuanced reviewer. "It's not as noticeable a lot of the times because Gyeong-nam has a low-key personality, but in the scenes when those emotions come through, their absence is felt."

This critique touches on a common challenge for idol-actors: the transition from performing to acting. Stage presence and screen presence are related but distinct skills. The ability to project charisma to thousands of fans doesn't automatically translate into the intimate, internal work required for dramatic acting.

Specific scenes requiring deeper emotional range—confrontations with family, moments of grief or anger, vulnerable confessions—have been cited as areas where Jisoo's limitations become more apparent. Critics suggest these moments feel rehearsed rather than genuinely felt, pulling viewers out of the story's emotional reality.

The comparison to co-star Seo In-guk, a seasoned actor with over a decade of dramatic experience, also highlights the gap. While both lead a perfectly enjoyable show, In-guk's ease with emotional modulation and comic timing makes Jisoo's relative stiffness more noticeable in their shared scenes.

The Idol-Actor Debate

Jisoo's reviews feed into a broader ongoing debate about idol-actors in Korean entertainment. The pathway from K-pop to acting has become increasingly common, but the quality of results varies dramatically.

Success stories exist. IU has established herself as one of Korea's most respected actresses while maintaining her music career. D.O. of EXO has earned critical acclaim for multiple film roles. Suzy transitioned from miss A to become a leading lady in multiple hit dramas.

But for every success, there are cautionary tales of idol-actors whose performances distract from otherwise solid productions. The debate often centers on whether agencies prioritize marketability over actual acting training, pushing idols into roles before they're ready.

In Jisoo's case, both YG Entertainment and Netflix clearly bet heavily on her star power. The show's production values are high, the supporting cast is strong, and the writing aims squarely at accessible entertainment. The question is whether these supports mask or highlight Jisoo's own limitations.

Fan Response vs. Critical Consensus

As with many idol projects, the gap between fan response and critical assessment is significant. BLINK, BLACKPINK's devoted fanbase, has enthusiastically promoted "Boyfriend on Demand" and largely praised Jisoo's performance.

Fan communities have organized streaming campaigns, created compilations of favorite moments, and pushed back strongly against negative reviews. This support has contributed to the show's strong Netflix numbers and kept it trending on social media throughout its run.

"Jisoo is perfect as Gyeong-nam," reads a typical fan response. "The character is supposed to be reserved and guarded, and Jisoo plays that beautifully. Critics just don't understand the character."

However, some fans have offered more measured takes, acknowledging room for growth while supporting Jisoo's journey. "I love her and I'll watch anything she's in, but I also want to see her improve," wrote one fan on a discussion forum. "She has the basics—now she needs the depth."

This tension between unconditional fan support and desire for artistic growth reflects a common dynamic in K-pop fandom, where loyalty and criticism can feel mutually exclusive even when they shouldn't be.

What's Next For Jisoo

Following the completion of "Boyfriend on Demand," Jisoo has pivoted attention to her upcoming solo music activities. In interviews promoting the drama, she mentioned working on her first full-length album—a significant project for an artist whose solo debut in 2023 was limited to a two-track single album.

"Fans want a variety of content, and I want to give them everything," Jisoo said in a recent interview. "Music is still my foundation, but acting has opened new doors for me. I want to keep exploring both."

No new acting projects have been announced, but industry observers expect Jisoo to continue pursuing dramatic roles. Her marketability ensures opportunities will continue to come; the question is whether she'll take on projects that challenge her rather than simply capitalize on her existing strengths.

For now, "Boyfriend on Demand" stands as a commercial success that leaves artistic questions open. Jisoo has proven she can carry a show; whether she can truly elevate one remains to be seen.

"Boyfriend on Demand" is available to stream on Netflix.
