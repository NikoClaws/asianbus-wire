Former Idol Hyogyeong Spilling Tea On Debut Debt And Disbandment Reality

March 17, 2026 By Julian Choi 📖 9 min read

Former K-pop idol Hyogyeong has sent shockwaves through the entertainment industry by candidly revealing the harsh financial realities of her idol career, including the crushing "debut debt" that followed her into disbandment and the traumatic aftermath of her group's unceremonious end. Her revelations have sparked renewed discussions about the exploitative aspects of the K-pop trainee system and the lack of support for idols after their groups dissolve.

In a lengthy YouTube video and subsequent interview with Korean media, Hyogyeong, who debuted with a girl group under a mid-tier agency in 2019 before disbanding in 2022, laid bare the financial structures that govern idol life. Her frank discussion has resonated with many current and former idols, some of whom have reached out to share similar experiences.

"I want people to understand what actually happens behind the scenes," Hyogyeong explained in her video. "Fans see the performances, the music videos, the glamour. They don't see the debt that accumulates from the moment you sign your trainee contract, or what happens to you when your group ends and you're left with nothing but bills."

The Debut Debt System Explained

At the heart of Hyogyeong's revelations is the "debut debt" system that pervades much of the K-pop industry, particularly at smaller agencies. Under this system, the costs of trainee training, living expenses, choreography, vocal lessons, and ultimately debut preparations including music production, music video filming, styling, and promotion are all advanced by the company and then treated as debt that idols must repay.

Hyogyeong revealed that by the time her group debuted, each member owed the company approximately 300 million Korean won (roughly $225,000 USD). This debt needed to be paid back from their share of earnings before they could receive any personal income.

"The numbers are structured so that you basically can never pay it off," she explained. "Your 'share' of earnings is usually something like 10-20% after the company takes their cut and deducts expenses. But new expenses are always being added—comeback costs, styling, accommodations. The debt kept growing even as we were supposedly earning money."

"In three years as an active idol, I never received a single won in actual salary. Everything I 'earned' went toward debt that seemed impossible to ever fully repay. I worked 18-hour days for years and ended up with nothing." — Hyogyeong, in her YouTube revelations

The Training Period Trap

Hyogyeong described how the debt cycle begins long before debut. As a trainee, she was provided housing, meals, and training, all of which were carefully logged as expenses she would eventually owe. The longer someone trains, the more debt they accumulate before even having a chance to earn income.

"I trained for four years before debut," she shared. "That's four years of accumulating debt while working incredibly hard with no guarantee of ever debuting. Some trainees I knew trained for seven, eight years and never debuted. They still technically owed money when they were dropped."

She also revealed that the quality of trainee provisions was often poor despite the costs being charged at market rates. "We lived in cramped dorms, ate cheap food, and were given minimal supplies. But when I saw the accounting, they were charging us full market prices for everything. A meal that cost them 3,000 won to provide might be logged as 15,000 won in our debt."

The Disbandment Aftermath

Perhaps the most emotionally charged portion of Hyogyeong's revelations concerned what happened after her group disbanded. When the company decided to end the group due to underperformance, the members were essentially cast aside with no support system, no income, and—in many cases—still carrying substantial debt.

"One day we were practicing for a comeback that was supposedly happening in two months. The next day we were called into a meeting and told it was over," she recounted. "There was no warning, no transition period, no help finding our next steps. We were just... done."

The emotional toll was severe. Hyogyeong described falling into depression, struggling with identity after years of being defined by her idol status, and facing practical challenges like finding housing and employment with no work experience outside entertainment.

"I gave everything from age 15 to 25 to become an idol. When it ended, I had no education beyond high school, no work skills, no savings, and debt. I had to move back in with my parents and start from zero. Actually, less than zero because of what I owed."

The Debt Follow-Through

One of the most shocking revelations was that Hyogyeong's agency continued to pursue repayment of her debt even after disbandment. Despite the fact that she had no income and the company had effectively ended her ability to earn as an idol, they still expected payment.

"They sent collection notices. They threatened legal action," she said. "I was terrified. Here I was, traumatized, unemployed, and being harassed for money I supposedly owed from an experience that gave me nothing in return."

Hyogyeong eventually negotiated a settlement with the help of a lawyer, though she declined to specify the terms. She noted that many former idols are not aware of their legal options or cannot afford legal representation, leaving them vulnerable to aggressive collection practices.

Industry Response

The revelations have prompted responses from various corners of the entertainment industry. Some former idols have come forward to corroborate Hyogyeong's account, sharing their own experiences with debt systems and post-disbandment struggles.

"Everything she's saying matches my experience," wrote one former idol on social media. "I debuted in 2017, disbanded in 2020, and I'm still paying off training debt. They structured the contracts so we could never win."

Industry associations have offered more measured responses, acknowledging that problematic practices exist while noting that not all agencies operate the same way. Larger, more established companies often have more favorable contract terms, they note, while smaller agencies struggling to recoup investment on uncertain talent may resort to more exploitative structures.

Legal And Regulatory Perspectives

Entertainment lawyers have weighed in on the legal aspects of trainee debt systems, noting that while many practices are technically legal, they often exist in ethical gray areas. The power imbalance between young trainees and established companies makes truly informed consent questionable.

"These kids sign contracts at 14, 15, 16 years old, often with parental consent but without real understanding of what they're agreeing to," explained entertainment lawyer Choi Min Seo. "Years later, they're bound by terms they couldn't have fully comprehended."

Calls for regulatory reform have intensified following Hyogyeong's revelations. Proposed measures include caps on trainee debt, standardized contract terms, mandatory financial disclosure, and support requirements for disbanded groups.

The Psychological Dimension

Mental health professionals have noted that the experiences Hyogyeong describes can have lasting psychological impacts. The combination of intense pressure during active idol years, abrupt loss of identity at disbandment, and financial stress creates conditions ripe for depression, anxiety, and other mental health challenges.

"Former idols face a unique form of trauma," explained psychologist Dr. Park Yuna, who specializes in treating entertainment industry professionals. "They experience sudden loss of community, purpose, and public identity, often while still very young. Without proper support, recovery is extremely difficult."

Hyogyeong acknowledged her own mental health struggles and encouraged other former idols to seek help. "Therapy saved my life," she said. "I wish agencies would provide mental health resources, but when they won't, we have to find it ourselves."

Fan Response And Support

Fans of Hyogyeong's former group, many of whom had wondered what happened to the members after disbandment, have rallied around her revelations. Crowdfunding efforts to support her financially have raised significant amounts, while messages of encouragement have flooded her social media.

"We had no idea things were this bad," wrote one former fan. "We supported the group, bought albums, went to concerts. We thought our money was helping them. Finding out they never saw any of it is devastating."

The situation has also prompted broader discussions about ethical consumption in K-pop and how fans might better support artists' actual wellbeing rather than just commercial success.

What Hyogyeong Wants Now

Looking forward, Hyogyeong has expressed hope that her revelations will contribute to industry reform. She's not seeking to return to idol life but wants to advocate for current and future trainees who may face similar exploitation.

"I'm speaking out because younger versions of me are signing these contracts right now," she said. "If my story makes even one person reconsider, or pushes agencies to be more ethical, it will have been worth the pain of sharing."

She's also begun building a new career as a content creator, using her platform to discuss industry issues and share her journey of rebuilding after idol life. While the path forward remains challenging, she expressed gratitude for the support she's received.

"I lost years of my life chasing a dream that turned into a nightmare. But I survived, and I'm finally telling my truth. That's something they can never take from me."
