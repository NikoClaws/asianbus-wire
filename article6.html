"K-Pop Demon Hunters" Wins Oscar Amid Boos and BTS Controversy

March 17, 2026 By Julian Choi

The 98th Academy Awards delivered one of its most controversial moments in recent memory when "K-Pop Demon Hunters," a Korean-American horror-comedy, took home the Oscar for Best Original Song amid audible boos from the Dolby Theatre audience—a stunning display of discontent that has been directly linked to the film's perceived negative portrayal of characters resembling BTS members.

The win for "Demons in the Dark," performed by American artist Halsey with Korean rock band The Rose, represented a historic moment for Korean cinema's integration with Hollywood. However, the celebration was immediately overshadowed by the unmistakable sound of audience disapproval that rang through the venue as the winners approached the stage.

Television viewers watched in confusion as what should have been a triumphant walk to the podium became an uncomfortable procession through a divided room. The film's director, Korean-American filmmaker Daniel Park, appeared visibly shaken as he accepted the award, his hands trembling slightly as he held the golden statuette.

The BTS Connection: Why ARMYs Are Furious

The controversy stems from "K-Pop Demon Hunters'" depiction of fictional idol characters that critics—particularly BTS's global fanbase ARMY—argue were intentionally modeled after BTS members. The film features a seven-member boy band called "BEYOND" who become unwitting vessels for demonic possession during a world tour.

While the filmmakers have repeatedly insisted that BEYOND is not based on any real group, the similarities have been exhaustively documented by fans. The fictional group's formation story, member dynamics, and even physical characteristics of actors bear what critics call "undeniable resemblances" to BTS.

Most controversially, the film depicts the BEYOND members engaging in increasingly violent and disturbing behavior as the demonic influence takes hold. Several scenes show the fictional idols attacking fans, destroying property, and speaking in explicitly profane language—imagery that ARMYs argue deliberately tarnishes BTS's carefully cultivated wholesome image.

"This isn't just a movie. This is a deliberate attack on BTS dressed up as 'satire.' They took everything that makes BTS special—their bond with fans, their positivity, their messages of self-love—and twisted it into a horror spectacle. And now the Academy has rewarded it." — Statement from BTS ARMY Global Alliance

The Oscar Night Incident: Inside the Dolby Theatre

According to multiple attendees, the boos that disrupted the acceptance speech originated primarily from a section of the audience known to include several K-Pop industry representatives and artists. However, the disapproval quickly spread to other parts of the theatre.

Sources inside the venue described the moment as "surreal" and "unprecedented." One attendee, speaking on condition of anonymity, recounted: "When they announced the winner, there was this split second of silence, and then you could hear people actually booing. At the Oscars. I've been coming to this ceremony for fifteen years and I've never heard anything like it."

Director Daniel Park attempted to deliver his acceptance speech despite the disruption, thanking his collaborators and the Academy. However, his closing remarks—"Art should make people feel something, even if that feeling is uncomfortable"—were drowned out by increased audience noise.

ARMY's Months-Long Campaign Against the Film

The Oscar night incident was merely the culmination of a sustained campaign by BTS fans to discredit and delegitimize "K-Pop Demon Hunters." Beginning months before the film's release, organized fan efforts targeted every aspect of the production.

ARMYs flooded social media with negative commentary, organized mass one-star review campaigns on aggregator sites, and contacted film industry organizations to protest the movie's existence. Petitions demanding the film's withdrawal from Oscar consideration gathered over 2 million signatures.

The campaign extended to pressuring Academy members directly. Multiple voters reported receiving thousands of messages urging them not to support the film, with some describing the communications as "intimidating" and "harassment."

"I've been an Academy voter for twenty years. I've never experienced anything like the pressure campaign around this film. My social media was flooded. I got emails. People somehow found my office address. All because of a movie nomination. It was genuinely disturbing." — Anonymous Academy member speaking to Variety

The Film's Defenders Strike Back

Supporters of "K-Pop Demon Hunters" have pushed back forcefully against the ARMY narrative, arguing that the film is legitimate artistic expression that does not deserve to be censored or suppressed by fan armies.

Director Daniel Park, a second-generation Korean-American, has defended his film as a commentary on the dark side of idol culture rather than an attack on any specific group. "The film explores what happens when we put human beings on pedestals and demand perfection from them," he explained in a pre-Oscar interview. "The horror elements are metaphorical."

Film critics have largely praised the movie, with many highlighting its sophisticated examination of parasocial relationships and the mental health impacts of extreme fame. The film currently holds a 78% critics score on Rotten Tomatoes, though its audience score sits at a dismal 12%—a disparity widely attributed to fan review-bombing.

The Broader Debate: Fan Power vs. Artistic Freedom

The "K-Pop Demon Hunters" controversy has ignited fierce debate about the appropriate limits of fan activism and the potential chilling effects on artistic expression. Industry observers note that the K-Pop fandom's organized might, while often directed toward positive goals, can also function as a censorship mechanism.

"What we're seeing is a new form of cultural power," explained media studies professor Dr. Jennifer Walsh. "These fandoms have the numbers and organization to significantly impact what gets made, what gets distributed, and what gets recognized. That's not inherently bad, but it raises questions about whose voices get heard in cultural production."

Others argue that fans have every right to advocate against content they find offensive or harmful. "If a film had depicted another community in the way this film depicts K-Pop fans and artists, we wouldn't question the backlash," countered cultural critic Kim Seung-hyun. "ARMYs are simply exercising their consumer power."

BTS and HYBE's Official Silence

Throughout the controversy, BTS themselves and their agency HYBE have maintained strict silence, declining to comment on the film or the fan campaign against it. This silence has been interpreted variously as tacit approval of fan actions, strategic distancing from the controversy, or simply prudent legal caution.

Industry analysts note that any official BTS statement could have significant implications. Endorsement of the campaign could expose the group to accusations of trying to suppress legitimate criticism, while condemnation of fan behavior could alienate their dedicated base.

"The smart play is exactly what they're doing—staying out of it entirely," suggested entertainment strategist Park Ji-won. "Let the fans fight this battle while maintaining plausible deniability. It's the safest path."

Aftermath: Divided Reactions Continue

In the days following the ceremony, reactions have remained sharply divided along predictable lines. ARMYs have declared the booing incident a moral victory, proof that the world stands with BTS against what they characterize as exploitation. Film industry figures have expressed concern about the precedent set by disrupting the ceremony.

The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences released a brief statement emphasizing "respect for all creative voices" but declined to comment on the specific incident. Privately, some Academy members have expressed frustration that the ceremony was overshadowed by what they view as an external culture war.

What Happens Next

The immediate aftermath of the controversy remains uncertain. "K-Pop Demon Hunters" is expected to see a significant box office bump from the Oscar win, though whether that translates to actual ticket sales or simply increased attention remains to be seen. The film's streaming debut is scheduled for next month.

For the broader K-Pop industry, the incident raises questions about how Korean entertainment's global expansion intersects with Hollywood power structures. As K-Pop continues to grow in international influence, similar conflicts seem increasingly likely.

And for BTS and their fans, the Oscar controversy represents another chapter in the ongoing negotiation between artist image protection and the limits of fan intervention. Whether this incident will lead to any recalibration of those boundaries—on either side—remains an open question.
