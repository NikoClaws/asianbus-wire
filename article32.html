National Pension Service Flooded With 1,500 Emails In 2 Hours Over ENHYPEN Heeseung

March 18, 2026 By Daniel Park 📖 8 min read

The fallout from ENHYPEN member Heeseung's sudden departure from the group has taken an unprecedented turn, with Korea's National Pension Service revealing that their international support center was paralyzed by a massive influx of calls and emails from overseas fans. The government agency, which manages retirement funds and provides crucial services to foreign workers and overseas Koreans, received approximately 1,500 emails within just two hours and experienced a flood of international phone calls that temporarily disrupted normal operations.

The chairman of the National Pension Service confirmed the situation in a statement released on March 18, describing how the Support Center had been overwhelmed by what appears to be a coordinated protest campaign by international ENHYPEN fans, known as ENGENEs. The chaos reportedly began after contact information for the international pension support center was shared widely on social media platforms, accompanied by posts urging fans to demand answers about HYBE's decision regarding Heeseung.

The situation has sparked a heated debate within the K-pop fan community about the ethics and effectiveness of such protest tactics, with many fans—including some Heeseung supporters—questioning whether targeting a government service that helps vulnerable populations is an appropriate way to express dissatisfaction with entertainment industry decisions.

Why The National Pension Service?

The connection between Korea's pension fund and HYBE Entertainment lies in the National Pension Service's role as a major institutional investor. As one of the world's largest pension funds, the NPS holds significant stakes in numerous Korean companies, including HYBE. This shareholder relationship led some fans to view the pension service as a potential avenue for pressuring HYBE's corporate decisions.

Social media posts that circulated before the incident reportedly suggested that because the NPS is a major shareholder in HYBE, fans could theoretically influence company decisions by pressuring the pension fund. The posts questioned whether the NPS was aware of the situation in advance and whether they understood the impact of HYBE's decision on fans and the artist.

However, as the NPS chairman firmly clarified in response to the flood of communications: "The National Pension Service is a long-term investor managing the public's retirement funds and does not interfere in the management or personal matters of individual companies. The composition of K-Pop members is also not a subject of our involvement."

The Human Cost Of Fan Activism

Perhaps the most troubling aspect of the incident is the collateral damage to people who actually need the pension service's help. The international pension support center exists specifically to assist foreign workers in Korea navigating the pension system and Koreans living abroad who need support with their retirement benefits.

With the support center's phone lines jammed and staff forced to sort through thousands of emails to identify legitimate inquiries, people with genuine pension-related needs faced significant delays. The NPS noted that some staff members had to be reassigned from their regular duties to handle the situation, potentially affecting service across other departments as well.

"People who actually needed help with their pensions were being delayed. Meanwhile, employees who have nothing to do with the situation have to sort through thousands of emails just to make sure they don't miss important ones." — K-pop fan on social media, criticizing the protest

The pension support center serves some of South Korea's most vulnerable residents—foreign workers who may not speak Korean fluently and elderly overseas Koreans who depend on their pensions for survival. Disrupting these services, even unintentionally, raises serious ethical questions about the methods some fans are willing to employ.

Fan Community Divided

The backlash from within the fan community has been swift and substantial. Many ENGENEs, including some of Heeseung's most devoted supporters, have spoken out against the pension service protest, arguing that it harms innocent people and could generate negative publicity that ultimately hurts the artist they're trying to support.

"I think ENGENEs need to reconsider when calling the National Pension Service as they do help with retirement funds, managing disability benefits, and overall helping low-income individuals and families," wrote one fan on Twitter. "There are other ways to still protest and this shouldn't be at the expense of others."

Another fan expressed frustration more bluntly: "Seriously, guys. They handle pension services for people who rely on them—retirees, elderly, workers, and families. What exactly are you trying to do here? And those spamming the communications office, have you no shame?"

The incident has reignited longstanding debates about the boundaries of fan activism in K-pop. While fans have increasingly embraced their collective power to influence industry decisions through organized campaigns, questions persist about when such activism crosses ethical lines.

The Heeseung Situation: A Recap

For those following the situation, Heeseung's departure from ENHYPEN was announced suddenly by BELIFT LAB (the HYBE subsidiary that manages the group), catching fans off guard and sparking widespread outcry. The specifics of why Heeseung is leaving remain somewhat unclear, with the official statement citing vague reasons that have done little to satisfy fans seeking transparency.

The "ENHYPEN Is 7" movement that emerged in response has taken various forms, from hashtag campaigns and streaming protests to the more controversial tactics like the pension service incident. Fans have expressed frustration not only with the decision itself but with what they perceive as a lack of adequate communication from the company.

The situation is further complicated by the fact that Heeseung himself has made limited public statements, leaving fans to speculate about his own feelings and wishes. Fan sign event appearances have been adjusted, with full refunds being processed for events he won't attend—a practical necessity that nonetheless serves as a painful reminder of the situation.

Corporate Responses And Industry Implications

Neither HYBE nor BELIFT LAB has directly addressed the National Pension Service incident, though industry observers note that such controversies rarely prompt official responses from entertainment companies. The companies' silence on this particular development may be strategic, as acknowledging the protest could inadvertently encourage similar tactics in the future.

However, the incident may have broader implications for how K-pop companies approach major announcements that affect group compositions. The intensity of fan reactions—extending now to disrupting government services—demonstrates the stakes involved when decisions are perceived as being made without adequate fan consideration or transparency.

"This is a wake-up call for the industry," suggested entertainment industry consultant Kim Soo Jin. "Companies need to invest more in communication strategies for sensitive announcements. When fans feel blindsided and powerless, some will resort to increasingly extreme measures to be heard. Better transparency upfront could prevent situations like this."

The Limits Of Shareholder Activism

The misguided focus on the National Pension Service also reveals fundamental misunderstandings about how institutional investment and corporate governance actually work. While the NPS is indeed a significant HYBE shareholder, pension funds typically do not engage in the kind of micromanagement that fans were apparently hoping to trigger.

Institutional investors like pension funds focus on long-term financial performance and may engage with companies on major governance issues, but decisions about individual idol group memberships fall far outside their purview. Even if the NPS wanted to influence such decisions—which they clearly don't—their investment mandate and fiduciary duties would prevent such involvement.

"The fans' logic was fundamentally flawed," explained finance professor Lee Jae Hoon. "Pension funds have a legal obligation to maximize returns for their beneficiaries—retirees and future retirees. They cannot and should not be making decisions based on K-pop fan petitions. The entire premise of this protest was built on a misunderstanding."

What Comes Next

As the dust settles from this particular incident, attention is likely to return to the core issues that sparked it: Heeseung's future, ENHYPEN's path forward, and the relationship between K-pop companies and their increasingly activist fanbases.

For the National Pension Service, operations have reportedly returned to normal following the initial surge, though staff remain on alert for any further incidents. The agency has not indicated any intention to pursue action against those who flooded their systems, likely recognizing that the participants were predominantly international fans beyond Korean jurisdiction.

Within the fan community, the incident may serve as a turning point in discussions about protest ethics. Several prominent fan accounts have called for a shift toward more constructive forms of activism—streaming campaigns, donation drives in Heeseung's name, or coordinated feedback to HYBE through appropriate channels.

"We can be loud without hurting innocent people," wrote one influential fan account. "Heeseung wouldn't want his name associated with disrupting services that help the elderly and foreign workers. We need to be better."
