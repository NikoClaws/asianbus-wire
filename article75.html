Netflix's "BTS: The Return" Documentary Reveals Band's Private Journey Making "ARIRANG"

March 23, 2026 10 min read By AsianBus News Staff

They live in the moment, shoulder their responsibilities, and "Swim" forward together with their brothers. That's the essential message of "BTS: The Return," Netflix's upcoming documentary that goes behind the scenes of the world's biggest K-pop group as they create their highly anticipated comeback album "ARIRANG."

Directed by Bao Nguyen, the documentary digs into sides of BTS that remain largely unknown even 13 years after their debut. Rather than focusing on spectacle and stage performances, the film offers an intimate look at the musical agony and pressure that come with holding the title of world's biggest K-pop group—and returning after nearly four years away from group activities.

Warning: This review contains mild spoilers for "BTS: The Return."

Roommates Again

A large part of the film unfolds during the members' two-month stay together in Los Angeles, where they gathered to work on "ARIRANG." For a band that began its journey living under one roof but has long moved into separate houses, the return to being roommates again feels both unfamiliar and deeply nostalgic.

Stripped of the grandeur of stadiums and global stages, the documentary lingers on small, intimate moments: the members swimming, playing volleyball, or simply laughing together. In one particularly memorable scene, Jimin dives into the ocean, prompting carefree laughter from the others.

It's through these unscripted fragments that the documentary quietly makes its point—that behind the global phenomenon, they are still, after all, just ordinary young men. The contrast between their public personas and these private moments is striking, revealing the human beings beneath the idol polish.

Around The Dining Table

That sense of intimacy continues around the dining table, where the members gather to share meals and speak candidly about their thoughts and concerns regarding the album. Conversations flow over drinks like soju, revealing raw and unfiltered emotions—moments of doubt, reflection, and mutual reassurance.

These scenes don't dramatize their struggles but instead present them with disarming honesty. For viewers outside the fandom, these moments may be the most striking. In such raw and intimate scenes, a sense of relatability emerges—a reminder that these globally-known stars are, in many ways, just like us, each carrying their own burdens and pushing forward through hard work.

The documentary captures the unique dynamic of seven individuals who have spent over a decade together, navigating fame, fortune, and the pressures that come with being cultural icons. Their ability to be vulnerable with each other, to express fears and frustrations openly, speaks to the depth of their bond.

Brotherhood As Foundation

The film underscores what has always been BTS's defining characteristic: brotherhood. It seems evident that this bond is what allows BTS to move—or, as the band phrases it in one of their new songs, "Swim"—forward together, balancing authenticity with the immense responsibility of representing not only themselves but also all of Korea on a global stage.

Director Bao Nguyen captures this dynamic with sensitivity, allowing the relationship between members to speak for itself rather than imposing external narrative. The result is a portrait of a group that functions as a second family, complete with all the complications and comfort that implies.

The Pressure Of Return

At the same time, the documentary doesn't shy away from the pressures that accompany BTS's position. The process of building a long-awaited album in the face of intense global anticipation proves anything but easy.

The members are seen revisiting ideas, questioning choices, and holding one another to high standards, revealing a level of rigor that often goes unnoticed by the public. The creative process is messy, frustrating, and uncertain—a far cry from the polished final product that fans will eventually hear.

Watching how individual songs evolved, like "Body to Body" and "Swim," becomes an interesting journey for viewers. The documentary shows tracks in various stages of development, from rough demos to finished productions, illustrating the amount of work that goes into creating music at BTS's level.

The Home Video Aesthetic

There's also an intimate layer to the film achieved through technical choices. Through the members' self-shot footage captured on a camcorder—what the director describes as "home video" vibes—the audience is offered a more personal perspective, one that feels less like observation and more like participation.

These unpolished moments further dissolve the distance between artist and viewer. When Jungkook films his bandmates goofing around, or when V captures a sunset, viewers feel like they're part of the group's inner circle rather than passive observers of a documentary.

The contrast between professional cinematography and these handheld, personal moments creates a rich visual texture that mirrors the documentary's thematic exploration of public versus private selves.

Beyond The Fandom

While "BTS: The Return" will undoubtedly satisfy ARMY's desire for new content, its appeal extends beyond the dedicated fanbase. The film presents something perhaps unexpected to non-fans: a chance to reconsider what lies behind the image of a K-pop star and to see, within it, something profoundly human.

The documentary doesn't require prior knowledge of BTS or K-pop to be engaging. At its core, it's a film about creativity, collaboration, and the bonds that sustain people through challenging times. These themes are universal, even if the specific context—global pop stardom—is not.

For those who have dismissed BTS as mere pop culture phenomenon without substance, "The Return" offers a compelling counterargument. The dedication, artistry, and genuine friendship on display complicate any simplistic narrative about manufactured K-pop stars.

Director's Vision

Director Bao Nguyen brings an outsider's perspective that proves valuable. As a Vietnamese-American filmmaker known for documentaries about cultural subjects, he approaches BTS with curiosity rather than reverence, asking questions that a more embedded director might not think to pose.

In interviews promoting the film, Nguyen has spoken about his goal of capturing "the unknown sides of BTS" that remain hidden even after 13 years of near-constant documentation. The fact that he found such sides to explore speaks to both his skill as a filmmaker and the depth of his subjects.

The Verdict

Ultimately, "BTS: The Return" goes beyond simply documenting the process of making the album "ARIRANG." It offers a closer look at BTS as people and as individuals. The film succeeds because it trusts its subjects to be interesting without artificial drama or manufactured conflict.

For ARMY, it's an essential addition to the BTS documentary canon—perhaps the most intimate glimpse yet into the group's inner world. For everyone else, it's a well-crafted documentary about seven remarkable individuals navigating extraordinary circumstances with grace, humor, and genuine affection for one another.

Netflix's documentary "BTS: The Return" will be released on Friday, March 28, 2026.
