THE BOYZ Contract War Escalates: 9 Members Demand Termination From ONE HUNDRED

March 20, 2026 9 min read By Julian Choi

The K-pop industry is witnessing one of its most dramatic contract disputes in years. According to Yonhap News Agency, nine of THE BOYZ's current members have demanded contract termination with their agency ONE HUNDRED (formerly Cre.ker Entertainment), citing a litany of grievances including breach of trust, unpaid compensation, and alleged misuse of group housing funds.

ONE HUNDRED has formally rejected the termination request, setting the stage for a protracted legal battle that could determine the future of one of K-pop's most accomplished boy groups.

The Breakdown

THE BOYZ debuted in 2017 as an eleven-member group under Cre.ker Entertainment. After former member Hwall's departure in 2019 and the 2022 removal of member Ju (amid unrelated controversy), the group currently operates with nine active members: Sangyeon, Jacob, Younghoon, Hyunjae, Juyeon, Kevin, New, Q, and Eric.

All nine remaining members have now filed for contract termination—a unified front that suggests deep-seated frustration with agency management.

The Allegations

According to legal filings obtained by Korean media, THE BOYZ members allege multiple contract violations:

  • Unpaid Compensation: Members claim they have not received full payment for recent activities, including their Japanese promotions and domestic concerts
  • Dorm Fund Misuse: The group allegedly contributed to a shared housing fund that was used for purposes other than their living arrangements
  • Schedule Mismanagement: Members cite instances where they were not informed of schedule changes, resulting in missed appearances and professional embarrassment
  • Lack of Transparency: Requests for financial documentation and activity accounting were allegedly ignored or delayed
  • Career Development Neglect: Members claim the agency failed to pursue opportunities that would have advanced their individual and group careers

The filings paint a picture of a deteriorating relationship between artists and management—one that apparently reached a breaking point in recent weeks.

ONE HUNDRED's Response

In a statement released Wednesday, ONE HUNDRED categorically rejected the termination request:

"We have reviewed the members' claims and find them to be without merit. ONE HUNDRED has fulfilled all contractual obligations and will continue to do so. We reject the termination request and intend to enforce the existing contract terms."

The agency did not address specific allegations but suggested the dispute stemmed from "differing interpretations of contractual language" rather than any actual wrongdoing.

Industry sources indicate that ONE HUNDRED may pursue breach of contract claims against members who refuse to fulfill scheduled activities during the dispute period.

The Ju Precedent

The dispute is complicated by the lingering case of former member Ju (Joo Haknyeon), who was removed from the group in 2022 amid prostitution allegations. Ju was later cleared of all charges, but ONE HUNDRED still sued him for breach of contract.

Some fans have drawn parallels between Ju's treatment and the current members' grievances, suggesting a pattern of agency behavior that prioritizes contractual leverage over artist welfare.

"The member who left—Ju—due to controversy never actually left," one fan forum post explained. "He was kicked out without even getting a chance to defend himself. He was cleared of prostitution charges but 100 still sued him for breach of contract."

The Ju case remains ongoing and may be cited in the current members' legal arguments.

What Happens Next

Korean entertainment contract disputes typically follow a predictable pattern:

  1. Negotiation Period: Both parties attempt to reach a settlement, often with mediators
  2. Legal Proceedings: If negotiation fails, courts determine whether contract termination is justified
  3. Activity Freeze: Members cannot work with other agencies or release new music during proceedings
  4. Resolution: Either contract termination is granted, a settlement is reached, or members must continue under existing terms

The process can take months or even years, during which THE BOYZ's career momentum would effectively freeze.

Fan Reaction

THE B (THE BOYZ's fandom) has largely rallied behind the members, with hashtags like #FreeTheBOYZ and #ProtectTheBOYZ trending on Korean social media.

"We trust our boys. If all nine of them are doing this together, something must be seriously wrong at that company."

"THE BOYZ have given everything to ONE HUNDRED for almost a decade. If they're asking to leave, we support them completely."

Some fans have organized boycotts of ONE HUNDRED-related content and merchandise, hoping economic pressure will encourage the agency to negotiate.

Industry Implications

The THE BOYZ case reflects broader tensions in K-pop's contract landscape. While regulations introduced in 2017 were meant to protect trainees and idols from exploitative contracts, established groups often find themselves locked into long-term deals signed before they achieved success.

If THE BOYZ successfully terminate their contracts, it could embolden other groups facing similar frustrations. Conversely, if ONE HUNDRED prevails, agencies may feel emboldened to resist artist demands.

For now, nine talented performers and their millions of fans can only wait—hoping for a resolution that allows THE BOYZ to continue making music, whether with ONE HUNDRED or elsewhere.
