'Kanye West' Mask Prank Trend In Korea Sparks Global Racism Backlash

April 2, 2026 5 min read By Mina Park

SEOUL — A prank trend sweeping Korean social media has ignited a fierce international debate about racism, after Instagram videos showed people wearing masks depicting the face of rapper Ye — formerly known as Kanye West — to frighten elderly family members. One clip alone has racked up more than 8.9 million views.

The format is consistent across dozens of viral videos: a creator conceals their identity with long sleeves, a hood or hat, and a realistic mask depicting a Black public figure. They then knock on a door and film the panicked reaction when an unsuspecting family member — typically an elderly parent or grandparent — opens it. The screams and shock become the content.

'It simply sheds more light on how racist people are'

While many Korean viewers have treated the clips as harmless entertainment, the reaction from international commenters — and from Black viewers in particular — has been sharply critical. The trend has been called out for both targeting vulnerable elderly individuals and for the racial implications of choosing a Black face as a scare prop.

"Koreans wearing a Black man's mask or Kanye mask does not look like a prank or a joke," one 37-year-old Black commenter told Korean media. "It simply sheds more light on how racist people are towards my race."

Another international user wrote that "picking out a specific foreign face mask is very disrespectful," adding that there was "no humor" in terrifying one's own mother for views.

Experts point to virality and profit, not explicit malice

Korean academics have offered more measured assessments, suggesting the trend is primarily driven by financial incentives and the psychology of viral content rather than deliberate racial hostility.

"Someone started it as fun, and when the view counts were high, others followed," a sociology professor at Jeonbuk National University told Korea Times. He noted that far-right YouTubers have demonstrated how high view counts can replace primary income, and this trend appears similarly motivated by monetization.

A psychology professor at Dankook University suggested the mask selection reflects creators seeking maximum visual contrast with Korean features, which amplifies the startle response and nervous system arousal — key ingredients for engagement on algorithm-driven platforms.

Broader context: K-pop's complicated relationship with race

The mask controversy arrives at an awkward moment for Korean pop culture's global image. K-pop has spent years building massive international fanbases, including significant followings in Black communities that have long recognized the genre's debt to hip-hop, R&B, and Black musical traditions.

Previous incidents — including blackface appearances on Korean variety shows and cultural appropriation controversies involving K-pop idols — have prompted similar debates, though the industry has gradually become more aware of and responsive to such concerns.

Whether the mask trend represents something fundamentally different or is simply the latest iteration of a recurring pattern depends on who you ask. What's clear is that the videos, boosted by Instagram's algorithm into tens of millions of global feeds, have forced yet another uncomfortable conversation about race in a country whose cultural exports now reach every corner of the planet.
