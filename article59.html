Geopolitics Threatens K-Pop: Japanese Members Excluded From China Concerts As Tensions Rise

March 21, 2026 10 min read By AsianBus News Staff

The K-pop industry's ambitious expansion into Greater China has hit a geopolitical wall. In a troubling sign of how international tensions can affect the entertainment world, Japanese members of several K-pop groups have been conspicuously absent from recent concerts in China and Macau—raising alarm among fans and industry watchers about the future of cross-border entertainment.

The most high-profile example came last month when RIIZE, SM Entertainment's rising boy group, performed in Macau without one of their six members: the Japanese rapper Shotaro. The 24-year-old, who trained at SM Entertainment after initially pursuing a dance career in Japan, was notably missing from promotional materials and the concert itself, with no official explanation provided.

The Unspoken Problem

While neither SM Entertainment nor Chinese concert organizers have explicitly stated why Shotaro was excluded, experts link the pattern to deteriorating relations between Japan and China. Tensions have escalated over territorial disputes, historical grievances, and Japan's increasingly close security ties with the United States.

"Entertainment has always been caught in the crossfire of geopolitics, but what we're seeing now is particularly concerning for K-pop," said Dr. Lee Hye-jin, a professor of East Asian cultural studies at Seoul National University. "The industry has built its growth model on reaching all Asian markets. When political tensions force artists out of certain venues, that model breaks down."

The issue extends beyond RIIZE. Multiple K-pop groups with Japanese members have faced similar situations, though agencies have remained largely silent about the circumstances. Industry insiders speaking anonymously confirm that some Japanese idols have been advised by management to skip China stops on their tours to avoid potential incidents or online backlash.

A History of Political Pop Culture

This isn't the first time geopolitics has affected K-pop in China. The most significant previous incident came in 2016-2017, when China imposed an unofficial ban on Korean entertainment following South Korea's deployment of the THAAD missile defense system. K-pop groups were barred from Chinese television, concerts were cancelled, and Korean content was scrubbed from streaming platforms.

That "Korean entertainment ban" lasted for years and significantly impacted the industry. Groups like BTS and EXO saw their Chinese activities curtailed, while agencies scrambled to pivot toward other markets in Southeast Asia and the West.

What's different now is the targeted nature of the exclusions. Rather than banning all Korean entertainment, the current pattern appears to specifically affect Japanese members of Korean groups—a more surgical approach that allows Chinese audiences to continue enjoying K-pop while sending a political message.

The Shotaro Case

Shotaro's situation has become a focal point for discussions about the issue. The dancer, known for his exceptional skills and charismatic stage presence, joined SM Entertainment in 2020 and debuted with NCT before moving to RIIZE for its 2023 launch. His absence from the Macau show was immediately noticed by fans.

"We waited hours in line to see all six members," said one Chinese fan who attended the concert, speaking to local media. "When we realized Shotaro wasn't there, many of us were confused and upset. The company didn't tell us in advance."

SM Entertainment has not released any statement addressing Shotaro's absence specifically. The group's Chinese social media accounts promoted the concert with images featuring all members but removed Shotaro's image from posts closer to the event date—a pattern fans documented extensively online.

Impact on Other Groups

RIIZE is not alone. Several other K-pop groups with Japanese members have faced similar pressures:

IVE: The group, which includes Japanese member Rei, has reportedly been cautious about scheduling China activities. While no concerts have been announced, industry sources indicate discussions about potential mainland events have been complicated by member nationality concerns.

LE SSERAFIM: With two Japanese members (Sakura and Kazuha), the HYBE group faces particular challenges. Both members are among the most popular in the group, making their exclusion from any concert problematic both commercially and for fan expectations.

TWICE: The veteran JYP Entertainment group includes three Japanese members (Momo, Sana, and Mina), representing a third of the lineup. TWICE's China strategy has been notably quiet in recent years, with some observers attributing this partly to the complexity of their multinational roster.

The Industry Dilemma

K-pop agencies find themselves in an impossible position. China remains one of the world's largest music markets, and excluding it from touring plans means significant lost revenue. But bringing Japanese members into situations where they might face harassment or serve as flashpoints for nationalist sentiment presents its own risks.

"This is a business decision wrapped in a political problem," explained a senior executive at a major K-pop agency, speaking on condition of anonymity. "We want our artists to perform for all their fans. But we also have to consider their safety and the potential for incidents that could spiral out of control."

Some agencies have attempted creative solutions. Performing in Macau or Hong Kong rather than mainland China was once seen as a workaround, given these regions' different administrative status. But as the Shotaro situation shows, even these venues are no longer immune to pressure.

Fan Perspectives

The situation has divided fan communities along national lines, with some defending the exclusions as understandable given historical tensions and others condemning them as unfair discrimination against individual artists.

Chinese fans of Shotaro have been particularly vocal in their disappointment. "Shotaro has never said or done anything political," wrote one fan on Weibo. "He's a dancer who loves his craft. Why should he be punished for things that happened before he was born?"

Japanese K-pop fans, meanwhile, have expressed concern for their compatriots in the industry. "It's heartbreaking to see talented artists excluded because of their nationality," said one Japanese fan community representative. "These idols work so hard and then can't even perform for part of their fanbase."

Korean fans have largely watched the situation with concern, worried about how it might affect their favorite groups' careers and the industry's overall health.

Looking Ahead

With no resolution to the underlying geopolitical tensions in sight, K-pop agencies will likely continue navigating these challenges case by case. Some industry observers predict a gradual shift in trainee recruitment, with agencies potentially becoming more cautious about debuting Japanese members in groups intended for pan-Asian markets.

"It's a sad possibility, but agencies may start thinking about roster composition differently," said industry analyst Choi Min-seo. "The dream of borderless K-pop, where any talented person could debut regardless of nationality, may be giving way to harder calculations about market access."

For now, artists like Shotaro continue their careers under a cloud of uncertainty, never knowing which concert they might be asked to skip or which promotional activity might exclude them. It's a reminder that even in the world of glittering pop entertainment, the cold realities of international politics are never far away.

SM Entertainment did not respond to requests for comment on this story.
